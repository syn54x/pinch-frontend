# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Taylor is the first and, for now, only user — building Pinch as his own daily finance tracker. But it is architected and designed as a multi-user product from day one: once it clears his own bar, it goes public. Design for the discerning power user who already knows the best tools in the category; never assume single-tenant shortcuts or an audience that tolerates rough edges.

The user's context: reviewing their money — quickly, often, and with high expectations. The job to be done on any given screen is confirming or correcting work the AI already did, and understanding where their finances are headed.

## Product Purpose

Pinch is an AI-native personal finance tracker. Plaid syncs the transactions in; the AI does the bookkeeping. The center of gravity is AI, reinforced by a set of features that each remove manual labor: intelligent auto-categorization with a transaction review flow that feels fast and easy, Penny (the in-app AI assistant), net-worth tracking, and loan payoff estimation. Success looks like a review session that takes minutes, numbers the user fully trusts, and a forward view (net-worth trajectory, payoff dates) they can act on.

## Positioning

Serious money intelligence, zero busywork.

## Brand Personality

Confident, precise, effortless. The interface should feel premium and powerful — the register of high-end fintech, not a budgeting toy. Voice is calm and declarative: the app states what it found and what it means; it never nags, celebrates excessively, or hedges.

## Anti-references

- Mint-style cluttered, ad-tier finance apps.
- Generic SaaS dashboard grammar — hero metric cards everywhere, identical card grids.
- Spreadsheet-austere utilitarianism.
- Playful/cartoon budgeting apps (YNAB-adjacent cheerfulness).

Positive references, for contrast: Copilot.money is the north star — the most modern, attractive UI in the category, and a transaction review system that is the most user-friendly and requires the least manual labor. Monarch is respected, but Copilot's interface wins where they differ.

## Design Principles

1. **Show finished work, not chores.** The AI already did the bookkeeping; every screen presents completed work awaiting a quick confirm — never a to-do pile demanding labor.
2. **Review, don't bookkeep.** The fastest path through any flow is "confirm"; correcting is always one interaction away. Minimize keystrokes-per-transaction as a hard metric.
3. **Premium is restraint.** Powerful does not mean loud. Density, polish, and precision over decoration; accent color for state and action, never ornament.
4. **Look ahead.** Surfaces lead with trajectory — net worth direction, payoff dates, what's next — not just historical record-keeping.
5. **Earned familiarity.** Feel instantly trustworthy to someone fluent in Copilot, Linear, and Mercury-class tools. Consistency over novelty; delight in moments, not pages.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA contrast throughout. Full keyboard navigability on the transaction review flow — power users will live there. `prefers-reduced-motion` honored on every animation.
